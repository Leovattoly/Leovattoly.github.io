Research overview

My work sits at the intersection of machine learning and chemical process engineering. Emulsion polymerization produces the polymer particles behind coatings, adhesives, and biomedical materials, and the properties of the final product depend critically on how the reaction is run. I build deep learning methods that (i) design the reaction — predicting the operating conditions that will produce a desired particle morphology — and (ii) monitor the reaction, detecting deviations early enough to intervene.

Reverse engineering polymerization conditions with deep learning

Achieving a target particle morphology traditionally requires expensive trial-and-error experimentation. This project inverts the problem: given the morphology you want, a neural network framework predicts the reaction parameters — temperature profiles and monomer feed rates — needed to reach it. The approach was developed within the EU Horizon Europe CINEMA project in collaboration with POLYMAT (UPV/EHU) and presented at the 8th Machine Learning and AI in (Bio)Chemical Engineering Conference at the University of Cambridge. A first-author paper is under major revision at Applied Intelligence (Springer).

Early detection of process deviations

Faults in a semi-batch polymerization are usually confirmed only after the reaction ends — when the batch is already lost. This project formulates online monitoring as an early time series classification problem: classify a running reaction as normal or deviating from multivariate signals (reactor temperature, heat-release rate, monomer conversion) as early as a reliable decision allows. A hybrid static–dynamic input architecture combines these trajectories with known feed-rate and set-point profiles; across 300 evaluated configurations, the best models detect deviations with 90.5% accuracy after only ~23% of the batch time. To our knowledge this is the first application of early time series classification to emulsion polymerization.

Earlier work: streaming anomaly detection for healthcare

At the Indian Statistical Institute, Kolkata, I led the Distributed Cognitive System for Healthcare project, designing streaming anomaly detection models for real-time elderly care monitoring.
