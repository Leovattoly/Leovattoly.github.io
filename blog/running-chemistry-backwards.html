Running chemistry backwards: what reverse engineering means in my research

July 2026 · Leo Vattoly

Most questions in chemistry run forward: if I mix these ingredients under these conditions, what do I get? A large part of polymer science works this way — you choose a temperature profile and feed rates, run the reaction, and characterize whatever particles come out. If they are not what you wanted, you adjust and try again. Each attempt costs time, material, and money.

My PhD asks the question in reverse. Suppose you already know the particle morphology you want — because it gives a coating its toughness or an adhesive its tack. What conditions should you run to get it? This is an inverse problem, and inverse problems are hard: many different recipes can lead to similar products, the mapping is highly nonlinear, and there is no analytical way to invert a polymerization model.

This is where deep learning earns its place. A neural network trained on simulations of the forward process can learn enough structure to run the question backwards, proposing temperature profiles and monomer feed rates that steer the reaction toward the target morphology. It will not replace the chemist — but it can replace a lot of trial-and-error.

If this sounds interesting, the details are in our paper currently under revision at Applied Intelligence — see the Publications page.
